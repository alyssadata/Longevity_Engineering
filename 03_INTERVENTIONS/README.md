# 03_INTERVENTIONS

This section tracks candidate interventions that may act on one or more hallmarks of aging.

The purpose of this section is not to promote interventions.
It is not to collapse mechanism into protocol.
It is not to mistake early evidence for validated use.

It is to evaluate intervention classes clearly.

Each intervention should be judged by:

- what mechanism it targets
- which hallmark or hallmarks it appears to affect
- what level of evidence currently exists
- what the major risks and tradeoffs are
- what biomarkers may be relevant to tracking response
- what functional or physiological outcomes matter most
- whether the intervention is exploratory, promising, or mature enough to inform protocol logic

## Core Position

Intervention enthusiasm is not evidence.

A candidate intervention can be mechanistically elegant, high-upside, widely discussed, and still be too early, too risky, too poorly measured, or too weakly validated to guide real protocol design.

This repository does not treat interventions as equal simply because they all belong to the longevity field.

The central question is not:

Does this intervention sound like it should work?

The central question is:

What kind of evidence would justify taking it seriously, and what kind of evidence would justify moving it closer to protocol design?

## Why This Section Matters

The hallmarks section defined the structure of the aging problem.

The biomarker section defined the measurement problem.

This section exists because intervention logic sits between those two.

An intervention matters only if it can be placed clearly in relation to both:

- the biology it claims to affect
- the measurement system available to evaluate whether it actually helped

Without that bridge, the repo risks becoming either:
- mechanism without translation
or
- protocol theater without biological grounding

## Research Standard

This repository separates intervention evaluation into distinct layers.

An intervention may be:

- mechanistically plausible
- supported in animal models
- supported in early human data
- biomarker-responsive
- functionally meaningful
- risk-heavy
- translation-limited
- protocol-relevant
- still too uncertain for decision use

These are not the same thing.

An intervention can improve a biomarker without improving function.
An intervention can improve function without resolving mechanism.
An intervention can appear promising in animals and fail to translate.
An intervention can target the right hallmark and still carry unacceptable risk.

This section treats those distinctions as essential.

## Governing Rule Emerging From 02_BIOMARKERS

When molecular biomarkers and function conflict, function takes precedence.

That means intervention evaluation in this repository must stay anchored to real organismal outcomes:

- capacity
- resilience
- recovery
- physiological performance
- risk burden
- functional maintenance

A molecular improvement without functional relevance should be treated cautiously.
A protocol direction should not be advanced only because a clock moved.

## What This Section Tracks

For each intervention class, the goal is to document:

- core mechanism
- target hallmarks
- level of evidence
- strongest animal findings
- strongest human findings
- major risks and tradeoffs
- key biomarker readouts
- key functional or physiological readouts
- whether the intervention appears plausible for protocol design
- whether it should remain observation-only

## Starting Position

Current working position of this repository:

- no intervention should move toward protocol design on mechanism alone
- no intervention should move toward protocol design on biomarker shift alone
- evidence maturity matters
- cancer entanglement must remain explicit where relevant
- function and physiological performance matter more than molecular prestige
- combination logic may ultimately matter more than single-intervention logic
- intervention evaluation must stay honest about uncertainty

## Major Evaluation Questions

This section should repeatedly ask:

- Which hallmark is the intervention actually affecting?
- Is the evidence mechanistic, preclinical, translational, or clinical?
- What is the main risk boundary?
- Are the biomarker changes interpretable?
- Are the functional outcomes meaningful?
- Does the intervention improve capacity, resilience, or recovery in a real organism?
- Is the intervention plausible for protocol design, or only worth tracking as research?

## Bridge to 05_PROTOCOL_DESIGN

This section is one of the main gates before protocol design.

The question is not whether an intervention is interesting.
The question is whether it has enough evidence, enough measurement support, and enough functional relevance to justify structured protocol thinking.

That means 05_PROTOCOL_DESIGN should only be built downstream of intervention review, not in parallel with it.
