## Scope

This repository is an open research notebook on biological aging, longevity, and reversal hypotheses.

Its purpose is not to sell anti-aging claims or present speculative optimism as fact. Its purpose is to track the field clearly, identify what is actually supported, and map which interventions appear most structurally important.

This work treats aging as a multi-layer biological process involving interacting failures across genomic, epigenetic, cellular, metabolic, immune, and systemic levels.

---

## The Problem

Biological aging is not one mechanism.

It is a compounding process shaped by multiple interacting hallmarks, including genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, disabled macroautophagy, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication, chronic inflammation, and dysbiosis.

Current medicine is strongest at treating downstream disease. Longevity research asks whether upstream aging mechanisms can be measured, slowed, partially restored, or in some cases reversed.

---

## What This Repository Is Trying to Solve

1. Is there a dominant upstream driver of aging, or is aging inherently multi-causal?
2. Which interventions have the strongest evidence for slowing specific hallmarks?
3. Which interventions are promising but still too early for strong claims?
4. Which biomarkers are credible enough to track intervention effects over time?
5. What would a minimum viable longevity framework look like for a high-functioning adult who prioritizes cognition, physical capacity, and long healthspan over raw lifespan extension?

---

## Research Standard

This repository separates:

- established framework
- early human evidence
- strong animal evidence
- mechanistic speculation
- hype

No clinical claims.  
No self-experimentation claims.  
No “reversal” language without direct evidence.  
No intervention is treated as validated for human aging unless supported by credible human data.

## Candidate Research Directions

### 1. Partial Epigenetic Reprogramming

Core idea: some aspects of aging may reflect loss of cellular information rather than irreversible genomic destruction.

Research interest focuses on partial reprogramming approaches such as OSK, which attempt to restore youthful cellular function without full dedifferentiation.

Current read:
- structurally important
- high upside
- high risk
- still preclinical for broad rejuvenation claims

Priority: HIGH

---

### 2. Senolytic Clearance

Core idea: senescent cells accumulate with age, resist apoptosis, and contribute to inflammatory and tissue-level dysfunction through SASP signaling.

Current read:
- strong animal rationale
- early human feasibility data exists
- still not standard of care
- likely relevant as part of a multi-modal framework, not a complete answer by itself

Priority: MEDIUM-HIGH

---

### 3. mTOR Modulation and Repair-State Shifts

Core idea: chronic growth signaling may trade off against maintenance and repair. mTOR modulation, caloric restriction, and related interventions may improve healthspan by shifting cells toward repair-state behavior.

Current read:
- one of the strongest mechanistic areas
- robust animal evidence
- human longevity data still early
- fasting should be treated as protocol-dependent, not universally safe or universally beneficial

Priority: MEDIUM-HIGH

---

### 4. NAD+ Restoration

Core idea: NAD+ declines with age and is linked to mitochondrial function, DNA repair, and sirtuin-dependent processes.

Current read:
- biologically active
- plausible support pathway
- human anti-aging outcome data remains mixed and inconclusive
- should not be framed as a proven longevity solution

Priority: MEDIUM

---

### 5. Telomere Biology

Core idea: telomere attrition contributes to replicative limits and senescence, but direct intervention is complicated by cancer risk and telomerase biology.

Current read:
- mechanistically important
- too risky for casual intervention framing
- better treated as a core biology track than a near-term protocol track

Priority: MEDIUM

---

### 6. Systemic Rejuvenation via Circulating Factors

Core idea: blood-borne factors, plasma dilution, and related systemic interventions may influence tissue aging.

Current read:
- scientifically interesting
- translation to humans remains uncertain
- not ready for strong practical claims

Priority: LOW-MEDIUM
