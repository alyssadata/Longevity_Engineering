A few things are becoming clear across the hallmarks.

**The sensing problem may be more important than the damage problem.**

Across almost every hallmark, what degrades first is not the structure itself but the cell's ability to detect its own state and respond correctly. Nutrient sensing deregulates. Autophagic flux fails not at initiation but at completion. Chaperone response weakens before proteins are universally defective. Epigenetic drift is loss of instructional fidelity, not loss of the underlying sequence.

If that pattern holds, aging may be primarily a failure of adaptive capacity before it is a failure of structure. That reframes the intervention logic. You are not just trying to fix damage. You are trying to restore the system's ability to recognize and respond to damage.

**The cancer entanglement is not a coincidence.**

It keeps appearing across telomeres, genomic instability, epigenetic reprogramming, and proteostasis. The systems that maintain longevity appear to be the same systems that suppress cancer. They may be in fundamental tension. Aging may partly be the price of cancer suppression. That is not widely stated this directly but the pattern across these hallmarks supports it.

**Epigenetic reprogramming is structurally unique.**

Every other intervention targets one hallmark at a time. Epigenetic reprogramming potentially resets the reading layer that governs all of them. That is why it keeps appearing across every file. It is not one intervention among six. It may be the only candidate that operates at the level above the hallmarks rather than within them.

**The measurement problem is underneath everything.**

Every hallmark has incomplete, tissue-specific, context-dependent biomarkers. That means any intervention framework built on tracking is sitting on an uncertain measurement base. This belongs explicitly in 02_BIOMARKERS as a foundational problem, not just a limitation.
